From Russia to Ghana: What Those Kids Taught Me About Language

👋 I'm Richard.

Before I started Speak Ghana, I spent time teaching English in a Russian kindergarten. I thought I was there to teach them. But they ended up teaching me everything I know about how people really learn a new language.

Summer in Russia, we took lessons outside. The kids loved watching videos together.
Actions speak louder than words. I learned to use my whole body to teach.
Inside the classroom, counting cookies was their favorite way to learn numbers.

When I was 26, I found myself in a Russian kindergarten. Twenty curious kids. Not a single word of English between them.

I walked in with flashcards and a lesson plan. I thought I knew what I was doing.

I was wrong.

By the time I left, those children had taught me more about language than any book ever could. And years later, when I started Speak Ghana, I built everything around what they showed me.

The Day Everything Changed

On my first day, I held up a flashcard of an apple. I pointed. I said "Apple." I said it louder. "APPLE."

The kids stared at me like I had three heads.

Then their teacher, Olga, came over. She crouched down next to a boy named Misha. She pointed to the apple and said slowly: "Яблоко." Then she pointed again and said: "Apple." Back and forth. She smiled. Misha smiled back.

Within minutes, Misha was saying "Apple" like he'd known it his whole life.

That's when it hit me: Language isn't taught. It's shared.

Three Lessons I Still Use Today

📖 1
Make it fun, and they'll remember

One of my favorite days was when we took class outside. The sun was out, and we sat in a circle watching an interesting video on a tablet. The kids were glued to it. They repeated every word. They didn't even realize they were learning, they were just having fun.

That's how we teach Twi at Speak Ghana. Games. Stories. Songs. Things that make you forget you're in a "class."

📖 2
Your body talks too

Another time, I stood in front of the kids and did big actions while I spoke. I pointed. I jumped. I made funny faces. They copied me. They laughed. And somehow, without translating anything, they understood.

That's why our Twi teachers use gestures, pictures, and real objects. Your brain remembers words better when your body is involved.

📖 3
Mistakes? No problem.

I remember teaching numbers inside the classroom one morning. A little girl kept calling "seven" something else. I corrected her gently, but she kept saying it wrong. I let it go.

One week later, she pointed at the number and said it perfectly. She just needed time. No pressure. No shame.

At Speak Ghana, we never rush you. We never make you feel bad for making mistakes. You'll get there. I promise.

Those Russian kids probably don't remember me. But I remember them.

I remember how their faces lit up when they said their first English word. The laughter. The games. The joy.

That's what I want for you. That feeling when you say your first Twi greeting. When you finally understand what your grandmother used to say. When you feel the language in your mouth and it feels like home.

What This Means for You

If you're in the diaspora and you've been wanting to learn Twi (or Ga, or Ewe, or Fante, or Dagbani), here's what I need you to know:

  • You don't need to be perfect. You just need to start.
  • It doesn't have to feel like school. We use games, stories, and real conversations.
  • You will make mistakes. That's how you learn. We'll be right there with you.

Come as you are. Whether you only know "Akwaaba" or you can already hold a simple conversation, we'll meet you where you are and walk with you from there.

Richard's Promise

When you join Speak Ghana, you're not just learning words. You're learning the way language was meant to be learned, with patience, with joy, and with people who believe in you.

The same way Olga taught Misha. The same way those Russian kids taught me.
